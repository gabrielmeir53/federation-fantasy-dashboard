\section{The Big Picture}

Every week, each team gets a \textbf{Power Rating} between roughly 35 and 65 (50 is average) in each league they play in. Your \textbf{Federation Power Rating} is the average of your ratings across all active leagues. Higher is better.

The rating is built from \textbf{six factors}, each measuring a different part of how well you're doing. Because different sports have wildly different scoring scales (NBA teams score $\sim$1200/week, NHL teams score $\sim$90), we convert every stat into a \textbf{$z$-score}---a number that says how many standard deviations above or below the league average you are. A $z$-score of $+1.5$ means ``way above average'' whether you're playing basketball or hockey.

\section{How the Score Is Calculated}

\begin{enumerate}[nosep, leftmargin=1.5em]
\item \textbf{Compute each factor} for all 12 teams in the league.
\item \textbf{Convert to $z$-scores}: for each factor, find the league average and standard deviation. Your $z$-score = (your value $-$ average) $\div$ standard deviation.
\item \textbf{Combine}: multiply each $z$-score by its weight and add them up.
\item \textbf{Scale}: multiply by 10 and add 50. That's your display score.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Example}: If your Win\% $z$-score is $+1.2$ and your PPG $z$-score is $+0.8$, just those two factors contribute $(0.35 \times 1.2) + (0.25 \times 0.8) = 0.62$ to your raw rating, which becomes about 6.2 points above 50 on the display scale.

\section{Federation Rating}

Your federation power rating is simply the \textbf{average} of your per-league ratings. If you're rated 58 in basketball and 52 in hockey, your federation rating is 55. Once a sport's season ends, that rating is \textbf{locked in} and stays in your average for the rest of the year.

\section{Key Takeaways}

\begin{itemize}[nosep, leftmargin=1.5em]
\item \textbf{Winning matters most} (35\% direct + 20\% via margin = $\sim$55\% of your score is about winning).
\item \textbf{Scoring matters, but it's not everything}. The old system was 80\% scoring. This one is 25\%.
\item \textbf{Context matters}. Beating good teams, being consistent, and trending upward all help.
\item \textbf{Cross-sport comparison works}. A score of 58 in basketball means the same thing as 58 in hockey: well above average.
\item \textbf{PR $\neq$ standings}. The power rating measures strength; the standings measure accumulated results. A team ranked high in PR but low in the standings may be due for a surge.
\end{itemize}

\text{PR}_i = 0.35\, z_{\text{WIN}}^{(i)} + 0.25\, z_{\text{PPG}}^{(i)} + 0.20\, z_{\text{MOV}}^{(i)} + 0.05\, z_{\text{CON}}^{(i)} + 0.10\, z_{\text{SOS}}^{(i)} + 0.05\, z_{\text{MOM}}^{(i)}
\]
\[
\text{Display Score}_i = 50 + 10 \times \text{PR}_i \qquad\qquad \text{Fed\_PR}_i = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{A}|} \sum_{L \in \mathcal{A}} \text{PR}_i^{(L)}
\]
\vspace{1pt}}}

\begin{tabular}{@{}ll@{\qquad}ll@{}}

$\text{WIN}$ & Win percentage & $\text{PPG}$ & Points per game (average) \\
